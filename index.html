Independent Research

The Illusion of Fit

A self-directed research project exploring cosmology, statistical modeling, and why disproving your own idea can be more valuable than proving it.

I had an idea about how the universe's expansion might work. I could visualize the concept, but I did not know the math well enough to test it, so I started learning. I worked through the math, built a computational model, and tested it against real astronomical observations.

At first, the results looked almost too good. Digging deeper revealed the truth: the model was too flexible and was fitting the noise, not the universe. The best outcome was not proving myself right. It was learning why my idea did not work — and how to test ideas the right way.

FEATURED BUILD

Donkey: Autonomous Vertical Landing Rocket Prototype

I figured if Elon Musk could land rockets, I probably couldn't — but I could at least give it a proper college try.

The idea

Donkey started as a simple question: what would it actually take to build a model rocket that could control itself in flight and attempt a powered vertical landing? The project turned into a hands-on systems engineering challenge involving embedded programming, electronics, CAD, 3D printing, fabrication, and a lot of trial and error.

I named the rocket Donkey after the character from Shrek because it was small, a little ugly, deeply stubborn, and for a brief moment, it would fly.

What I taught myself

  • Arduino programming and flight-control logic
  • GPS, altitude, servo, and sensor integration
  • PCB soldering and electronics prototyping
  • CAD design and 3D-printed thrust vector control
  • Welding, fabrication, and physical test iteration
01

Flight Computer

Arduino-based control system integrating GPS, sensors, servos, and supporting circuitry.

02

Thrust Vector Control

CAD-designed and 3D-printed mechanism to redirect thrust during flight.

03

Landing System

Custom landing mechanism and ignition sequence concept for controlled descent.

04

Fabrication

Hands-on welding, assembly, wiring, test fitting, and prototype iteration.

“The value of this project was not that it was perfect. It was that every obstacle forced me to learn a new discipline, connect it to the rest of the system, and keep iterating.”
